# The Hacker View

The hacker's experience in IDApTIK is grounded in **physical access to hardware**. You don't start with a god-mode view of all systems—you start with whatever device you have access to, and work from that machine's perspective.

## Physical-First Access

The hacker exists in the game world. To hack, you need hardware:

- **Your own device** – Bring a laptop to a cafe, use your phone, work from your apartment
- **Corporate devices** – Access a workstation in an office you've infiltrated (hope no one notices)
- **Server room terminals** – The infiltrator got you physical access to the good stuff
- **Opportunistic finds** – Rarely, you might find a device left logged in (lucky break)

Your physical location matters. Guards can find you. Cameras can spot you. If you trigger a building-wide power cut, you might black out your own terminal. Hacking from a cafe is safer than hacking from inside the target building—but you can only reach what's accessible from the outside.

## Device Interfaces

All interfaces are **stylized** for gameplay—not realistic OS simulations.

### Basic Devices (Servers, Routers, Cameras, etc.)
A simple, clean GUI inspired by _Uplink_:
- Login screen with user selection (admin, read-write, read-only, or specific usernames)
- File browser for viewing/editing files and logs
- Basic controls relevant to the device type (camera feeds, routing tables, service toggles)
- Terminal/CLI available for those who prefer it or need advanced operations

This ensures accessibility—players uncomfortable with command lines can still interact with servers and network equipment through point-and-click.

### Personal Computers (Laptops, Desktops)
Fuller graphical interfaces with:
- Desktop environment with apps and file management
- Multiple applications (terminal, file browser, network tools, text editors)
- More visual feedback and interactive elements

### CLI Access
Command-line interfaces are available on most devices for players who want speed, scripting, or direct control. It's a tool, not a requirement.

## Getting Online

Most static devices (desktops, servers) are already connected to their local network and potentially the internet. For portable devices you bring:

- **Public WiFi** – Click the network name, you're in. Simple, but your location is known.
- **Mobile data** – Your own connection, but traceable to you
- **Corporate networks** – If you're inside, you may need credentials found elsewhere

### The Online World

Once connected to the internet, you can access:

- **InterNIC** – A public registry of servers and their IP/hostnames (like a phonebook for the internet)
- **Known targets** – IPs discovered through files, logs, or intel gathered during the mission
- **Bounce points** – Compromised machines you can route through to hide your origin

**Bouncing is recommended.** Direct connections leave traces. Route through intermediary machines to obscure your trail.

## Network Views

### Local Network View
When connected to a local network, you see a **star topology** centered on the router/hub:

- Devices appear as nodes (workstations, servers, cameras, etc.)
- Each device shows its associated services (SSH, HTTP, camera feed)
- **Progressive reveal** – You don't see everything immediately. Scanning and exploration reveals more.

### Online Connection View
For internet targets, an **Uplink-style connection map**:

- Your machine at one end, target at the other
- Bounce points in between
- Visual representation of your connection path
- Trace indicator showing how close security is to finding you

## Discovery Methods

How do you find what's on a network?

1. **Network scanning** – Active probing reveals devices (but may trigger alerts)
2. **Progressive exploration** – Connect to devices, see what they can reach
3. **Files and logs** – Sometimes the best intel is a `passwords.txt` or a network diagram in someone's Documents folder
4. **Mission briefings** – Intel gathered beforehand may reveal key targets

Different missions emphasize different discovery methods. Sometimes you scan aggressively. Sometimes you're piecing together a network map from scattered breadcrumbs.

## Mission Goals

What you're trying to accomplish varies by mission:

- **Data extraction** – Find and copy specific files
- **System destruction** – `rm -rf /sys` on a database server
- **Backdoor installation** – Plant persistent access in an ISP or power station for future missions
- **Support role** – Access a camera system to pause feeds while the infiltrator slips by
- **Evidence planting** – Leave incriminating files on a target's machine
- **Covering tracks** – Delete logs, remove traces of the operation

The hacker's job adapts to what the mission needs.

## Future Considerations

### Base of Operations Progression (Not Yet Implemented)
Players may have a home base / secret hideout that serves as their starting point. Progression could include:
- Better hardware (faster hacking, more storage)
- Network infrastructure (dedicated lines, better anonymity)
- Physical security (harder for authorities to find/raid)
- Amenities that affect gameplay (planning boards, equipment storage)

This creates an incentive loop: successful missions fund base upgrades, which enable harder missions.
